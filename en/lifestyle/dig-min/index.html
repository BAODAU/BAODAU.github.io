Thoreau economics#

The cost of a thing is the amount of life required to be exchanged for it immediately or in the long run.

radical rethinking of the consumerist culture.

  • who could justify trading a lifetime of stress and backbreaking labor for better ‘stuff’
  • Thoreau’s new economics was devaloped in an industrial age, but his insigts apply to our life.
  • maintaining an active presence on Twitter open up an interesting new connection or expose me to an idea I hadn’t heard before. but we have to balance this porfit against the costs measured in terms of our life. how much of my time and attention that I have to sacrifice to earn the small profit of occasional connections and new ideas that is earned by cultivating a presence on Twitter. it would be way too high

costs compound#

  • combining different media platforms would result in a high cost of life. the return on investments of my presence on those social media is almost nothing. we have to treat the minutes of our life as a concrete and valuable substance.

the most valuable substance we possess. the cumulative cost of noncrucial things we clutter our lives with can far outweigh the small benefits each individual piece of clutter promises.#

Return Curve#

  • law of diminishing returns: at a high level, investing more resources into a process cannot indefinitely improve its output - eventually approach a natural limit and yield diminish return

  • consider the energy invested in trying to improve the value these processes return in my life. reading the news help me stay informed about current politicl climate, but at some point I’ll have to pay for this level of being informed with my attention, while gaining non-valuable marginal returns.

  • most people invest very little in these types of optimizations.

  • large attention economy companies dont wants us thinking about optimization. these corporations make more money the more time you spend engaged with their products. if i think these services as a collection features which i can individually find elsewhere. i

spend time alone#

  • dealing with people might be the biggest drain on your time and energy. alright im gonna digress so i can add my value by showing people that dealing with me will help them recuperate those precious those energy.
  • I can enjoy solidue in a crowded place. solitude can be disturbed in the form of reading a book, listening to a podcst, watching TV, or performing just about any activity. solitude requires me to move past reacting to information created by other people and focus instead on my own thoguhts and experiences.

All of humanity’s problems stem from man’s inability to sit quietly in a room alone. - Blaise Pascal-

I have read abundance of fine things on the subject of solitude… I acknowledge solitude an agreeable refreshment to a busy mind. - Ben Franklin-

  • Edward Gibbon lived a solitary life. but he was very productive and happy. A great deal of time alone is the common factor among creative people.
  • the ability to be alone is a rejection of close bonds. it helps build appreciation for interpersonal connections when they do occur.

We enter solitude, in which also we lose loneliness -Wendell Berry

Solitude deprivation#

  • oh my god. we allow headphones to provide background noise for our entire day. it undermines our time alone with our own thoughts.
  • Spotify provides me the ability to be CONSTANTLY distracted from my own mind. it is pushing us toward a newly alienated phase in our relationship with our own minds.
  • the smartphone provided a new technique to banish these remaining slivers of solitude: a quick glane
  • we’ve been sold the idea that more connectivity is better than less.
  • without solitude, I lose the ability to clarify hard problems, regulate my emotions, build moral courage, strengthen relationships. if i suffer from chronic solitude deprivation, the quality of my life degrades.
  • smartphones make you anxious.
  • humans are not wired to be constantly wired

practice 3 write letters to yourself#

dont click like??#

  • man is by nature a social animal.social cognition: when given downtime, our brain defaults to thinking about our social life.
  • people are naturlaly interested in their own social life.
  • social media makes me feel both connected and lonely, happy and sad. the more you use social media to interact with your network, the less time you devote to offline communication. there exists a tradeoff.
  • using social media tends to take pepole away from the real world socializing thats massively more valuable. the small boosts i receive from posting on a friend’s wall or liking their latest Ig photo can’t come close to compensating for the large loss experience by no longer spending real world time with that same friend.
  • IRL interactions require our brains to process large amounts of info about subtle analog cues such as body language, facial expressions, and voice tone. digital tools underutilize our social processing networks. humans are naturally biased toward activities that require less energy in the short term. even if it’s more harmful in the longterm. so we end up texting our sibling instead of calling them on the phone. or liking a picture of a friend’s new baby instead of stopping by to visit.
  • its difficult to reist checking a device in the middle of a conversation. our dumb brain cant distinguish betwee nthe importance of the person in the room with us and the new person who just us a new text.
  • novel tools are engineerd to hijack our primitive brain, having a very addictive allure. if i spend multiple hours a day compulsively clicking and swiping. theres much less free time left for slower interactions.
  • we are also delusional in thinking that we’re actually building or maintaining our relationships. making further action unnecessary.
  • conversation is what counts.
  • those whose relationship with you only exists on social media: let them go.

reclaiming conversation#

  • face-to-face conversation is the most human and humanizing thing we do. fully present to one another, we learn to listen. its where we develop the capacity for empathy. its where we experience the joy of being heard, of being understood.
  • young employees retreat to email because the thought of an unstructured conversation terrifies them, and uncessary office tensions that fester when communication shifts from nuanced conversation to ambiguous connection
  • f2f convo unfolds slowly. it teaches patience. we attend to tone and nance. when we communicate on our digital devices, we learn different habits.

not anti-technology. it’s pro-convo#

  • anything textual or non-interactive – all social media, email, text, instant message, doesnt count as conversation and should instead be categorized as mere connection.a logistical role.
  • as in. it should be used to set up and arrange conversation. or to efficiently transfer practical onfo. connection MUST BE a supporter to conversation.

real conversation takes time#

  • conversation is the good stuff. it is what we crave as humans and what provides us with the sense of community and belonging necessary to thrive
  • you cannot expect an app dreamed up in a dorm room, or among pingpong tables of a silicon valley incubator to replace the types of rich interactions to which we’ve painstakingly adapted over millennia. our sociality is simply too complex to be outsourced to a social network or reduced to instant messages and emojis.
