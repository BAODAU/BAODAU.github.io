prioritizing, but not pursuing, happiness#

chasing superiority#

  • as children, we are exposed to feedback that tells us whether we are superior or inferior to others around us.
  • consider the impact of failing to acquire a girlfriend or boyfriend when everyone else has one.

reasons we seek superiority#

  • we learn that valedictorians get better scholarships, CEOs get higher wages, and top entertainers get better deals and more magazine covers. we also learn about the importance of superioirty from TV and magazines, which selectively cover stories of successes rather than failures
  • we come to tether our self-esteem to being superioir. when we learn that the amount of love and attention we get from others depends on how well we stack up to our peers, we internalize the need for superiority to such an extent that we seek it even if the others aren’t around to judge us.
  • rooted in our desire to figure out whether we are making adequate progress toward cherished goals.

As humans, one of our most important goals is mastery: the desire to become increasingly competent or effective at something.

  • being ‘superioir’ gives us the autonomy or freedom to be who we are; higher-status individuals don’t feel the need to watch what they say or restric their behaviors.
  • superiority is about the need for approval. it is about self-esteem. it is aboyt mastery. and about autonomy.

does being superior make me happier#

  • yes.
  • participants made to feel superior displayed more positive expressions, while those made to feel inferior displayed more negative ones.
  • happy people are generally more successful.
  • lower in status are more prone to falling sick, are sick for longer periods of time, have shorter life spans, and suffer more emotional negativity than those higher in status.
  • those higher in status perceive themselves to have greater autonomy and control over their own decisions and this makes them happier

why materialism lowers happiness#

  • the catch with earning more is that expenses catch up with earnings.
  • we adapt to new levels of wealth, power, and fame means that we need to become increasingly wealthy, powerful, and famous over the course of our lives to maintain high levels of happiness.
  • adaptation is a big reason why materialism diminishes happiness levels in the long run.
  • promotes self-centeredness and lowers compassion, making others less likely to cooperate with materialistic people, leading them to be less happy in the long run.
  • lower compassion leads us to compromise on things that actually bring joy and happiness - things like haning out with freinds and family or contributing to society.
  • compared to nonmaterialistic people, materialistic people are more likely to suffer from mental disorders
  • tethering one’s self-worth to being better than others can lead to obsessing about the implications of failing to achieve this goal, making one more vulnerable to depression.
  • those with a high need for superiority may end up sacrificing long-term learning and growth for the sake of short term boosts in hubristic pride.
  • the detrimental effect it has on the quality of relationships. those who pursue superiority tend to be takes and takers aren’t well liked.
  • people are much more likely to cooperate with givers
  • the less attention you pay to how much better or worse than others you are, the happier you are likely to be. the more you compare yourself with others, the less happy you will be.

mitigating the need for superiority#

  • not only is the need for superiority hardwired in us, but most of us are also socialized to pursue it.
  • being superior makes us happier, pursuing superiority lowers happiness
  • mitigate the misconception about the need for superiority. many of us believe the need for superiority is an important determinant of success. this is mostly wrong.
  • why?? the need for superiority helps us get started. however, once we start a task, that needs becomes a hindrance.
