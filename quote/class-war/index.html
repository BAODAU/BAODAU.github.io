Class War

Excerpts from American Affairs’ The Real Class War. Note that this post is literally me taking notes (more like copying verbatim) of what the author wrote and I have absolutely no rights to the material provided. Don’t @ me.

  1. The working class has experienced economic stagnation and precarity, and even life expectancy in the US, as well as ower family stability and civic engagement. Social mobility has declined, while inequality has widened.
  2. [the working class] has scarecely any political agency in the current system and no apparent means for acquiring any. At most, working-class voters can cast their votes but can exercise no influence on policy formation, agency personnel, governance areas (that have been trasnferred to technocratic bodies?)
  3. While a restive working class might provide fertile ground for political upheavals, any fundamental transformation of Western politics will necessarily be led by increasing numbers of the “elite” who defect and rethink their allegiance to establishment paradigms. 4.Conventional narratives, including many that are critical of the status quo, paint the elite as a unified block aligned with neoliberalism. But the neoliberal economy has created a profound fracture within the elite, the significance of which is just beginning to be felt.
  4. The socioeconomic divide that will determine the future of politics, particularly in the US, is not between the top X percent and the rest (1% vs 99%). The real class war is between the 0.1 percent and (at most) the 10 percent. Precisely, between elites primarily dependent on capital gains and those primarily dependent on professional labor.
  5. At bottom, the economy that has been constructed over the last few decades is nothing more than a capital accumulation economy. As long as returns on capital exceed returns on labor, then the largest capital holders benefit the most, inequality rises, and wealth becomes more and more narrowly concentrated. Marxian thinkers have been analyzing theses dynamics for almost two centuries, but they have often misread the political effects of these developments, which play out primariliy among the elite managerial class, rather than within the binary of capitalists and proletarians.
  6. Lost amid the celabrations of the “information economy” in recent decades is the fact that elite career trajectories are no longer what they used to be.

Other professionals

  • fare worse

  • the conspicuous embrace of “elite values” by journalists and academics is often little more than an aspirational attempt to remain connected to an economically distant elite- just as educated millenials’ conspicuous consumption of “experiences” often serves as a necessary distraction from the grim reality that most will never be able to own a home.

The rising cost of elite membership

Facts

  • “low income” threshold in Norcal is ~ $117k

  • top 10% US household income threshold is $187k

  • what exists: deindustrialization and sectoral clustering, financialization and rising intra-elite inequality

  • global oligarchs buying investment properties and pieds-à-terre in prime urban areas to price out professional elites => displaced elites began gentrifying working-class and middle-class neighborhoods

  • the costs of passing elite status quo onto children-elite education- rose rapidly.

  • while the costs of education have risen, since 2000 the rewards have been declining. Wages for young college graduates actually feel.

upper middle class american radicals

  • everything mentioned above has a more pronounced impact on the professional elite than on the working class, which was already largely marginalized by that point
  • elites outside of the very top found themselves falling further behind their supposed cultural peers, without being able to look forward to rapidly rising incomes for themselves
  • members of the elite often seem far more radical than the working class, both in their candidate choices and overall outlook, lower-level elites appear to be experiencing far mor intense status anxiety
  • there is more “socialist” organizing in Silicon Valley and elite college campuses today than in working class exurbs. Mayy of the most aggressive proposals assoaciated with the Left- such as student loan forgiveness and “free college” are targeted at the top 30%. Medicare for All could potentially benefit households earning between $100k and $200k the most, cohorts below that are already subsidized. Whatever the underlying merits of “woke” critiques and causes might be, these postures are mainly adopted in intra-elite competition for positions and influence.
